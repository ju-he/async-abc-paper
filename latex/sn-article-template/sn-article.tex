\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}

Approximate Bayesian computation (ABC) is a standard tool for simulator-based inference when the likelihood is unavailable or too expensive to evaluate directly \cite{beaumont2019abc}. Sequential ABC methods improve efficiency by evolving proposal distributions and tolerance thresholds across a sequence of intermediate targets \cite{toni2009abcsmc,beaumont2009adaptive,delmoral2012adaptive}. In practice, however, many ABC-SMC implementations remain organized around synchronous populations, which introduces generation barriers and idle compute resources when simulation runtimes vary strongly across parameter settings.

This paper focuses on a steady-state asynchronous alternative designed for heterogeneous high-performance computing workloads and implemented within Propulate. The intended contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item a history-reconstructed \methodname{} algorithm compatible with Propulate's stateless propagator interface;
\item a practical formulation of monotone tolerance adaptation and rolling archive reconstruction from evaluated history;
\item an empirical evaluation against pyABC and other ABC baselines; and
\item an analysis of runtime heterogeneity, utilization, and scaling behavior on distributed systems.
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of the paper first summarizes the relevant ABC background, then develops the asynchronous method, describes the implementation, and finally lays out the experimental program that will populate the final results section.

\section{Background and Related Work}\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Approximate Bayesian computation}\label{sec:background-abc}

ABC replaces exact likelihood evaluation with simulation and discrepancy-based acceptance. For observed data $y$ and parameter $\theta$, the ABC target at tolerance $\epsilon$ can be written as
\begin{equation}
\pi_{\epsilon}(\theta \mid y) \propto \pi(\theta) L_{\epsilon}(\theta),
\label{eq:abc-target}
\end{equation}
where $\pi(\theta)$ is the prior and $L_{\epsilon}(\theta)$ denotes the likelihood approximation induced by the discrepancy threshold. The final paper should expand this section with the benchmark-specific discrepancy definitions, summary statistics, and tolerance schedules used in the experiments.

\subsection{Sequential ABC algorithms}\label{sec:background-sequential}

ABC rejection provides the simplest baseline but scales poorly in low-tolerance regimes. ABC-SMC and ABC-PMC address this by moving through a sequence of intermediate targets with adaptive proposals and importance weights \cite{toni2009abcsmc,beaumont2009adaptive,delmoral2012adaptive}. A common proposal form is the mixture
\begin{equation}
q_r(\theta) = \sum_{j=1}^{N_r} W_j^{(r)} K(\theta \mid \theta_j^{(r)}),
\label{eq:proposal-mixture}
\end{equation}
where $K$ is typically a Gaussian perturbation kernel around accepted particles from the previous stage.

The main limitation for distributed execution is that these methods usually advance in generations: the proposal distribution and tolerance update only after an entire population has been evaluated. On heterogeneous workloads this creates a synchronization bottleneck.

\subsection{Parallel and distributed ABC}\label{sec:background-distributed}

Existing software such as pyABC demonstrates that ABC can be distributed effectively across multi-core and cluster environments \cite{klinger2018pyabc,schalte2022pyabc}. The key distinction for this paper is not whether simulation is parallelized, but whether the inference algorithm itself requires synchronous population boundaries. That distinction motivates the event-driven formulation introduced next.

\section{Method}\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Design constraints}\label{sec:method-constraints}

The implementation target is Propulate, whose propagator interface exposes a single stateless call of the form \texttt{\_\_call\_\_(inds: List[Individual]) -> Individual}.
The propagator receives the full evaluated history and must produce the next candidate without relying on persistent archive state or asynchronous callbacks. This leads directly to a history-based reconstruction of all algorithmic state.

Let $\mathcal{H}_n = \{x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n\}$ denote the evaluated history after $n$ completed simulations. The algorithm reconstructs
\begin{align}
\epsilon_n &= T(\mathcal{H}_n), \label{eq:tolerance-functional} \\
A_n &= A(\mathcal{H}_n, \epsilon_n), \label{eq:archive-functional} \\
q_n(\theta) &= Q(\theta \mid A_n), \label{eq:proposal-functional}
\end{align}
where $T$ is the tolerance scheduler, $A$ is the archive selection rule, and $Q$ constructs the proposal distribution from the reconstructed archive.

\subsection{Tolerance reconstruction and monotonicity}\label{sec:method-tolerance}

Each proposed individual stores the effective tolerance active at proposal time. Given stored tolerances $\tau_i$, the effective tolerance reconstructed from history is
\begin{equation}
\epsilon_{\text{hist}} = \min_{i: \tau_i \neq \varnothing} \tau_i,
\label{eq:tolerance-history}
\end{equation}
with fallback to the initial tolerance when the history is empty. The scheduler proposes an updated tolerance $\epsilon_{\text{sched}}$, and the final tolerance is
\begin{equation}
\epsilon_n = \min(\epsilon_{\text{hist}}, \epsilon_{\text{sched}}).
\label{eq:tolerance-monotone}
\end{equation}
This gives a monotone decrease guarantee without mutable propagator state.

\subsection{Archive reconstruction}\label{sec:method-archive}

The active archive is reconstructed by filtering the evaluated history according to the current tolerance:
\begin{equation}
A_n = \left\{ \theta_i \in \mathcal{H}_n : \rho_i < \epsilon_n \right\},
\label{eq:archive-set}
\end{equation}
followed by truncation to the best $k$ particles by discrepancy. This archive approximates the current ABC target and supplies the particles used for proposal construction.

\subsection{Proposal and weighting}\label{sec:method-proposal}

With archive particles $\{\theta_j\}_{j=1}^{k}$ and normalized weights $\{W_j^{(n)}\}_{j=1}^{k}$, the steady-state proposal is
\begin{equation}
q_n(\theta) = \sum_{j=1}^{k} W_j^{(n)} K(\theta \mid \theta_j),
\label{eq:steady-state-proposal}
\end{equation}
using a Gaussian perturbation kernel with covariance estimated from the current archive. A newly proposed particle receives importance weight
\begin{equation}
w^\star = \frac{\pi(\theta^\star)}{q_n(\theta^\star)}.
\label{eq:importance-weight}
\end{equation}

The final manuscript should discuss the known approximation introduced by asynchronous execution: the proposal used to generate a particle is fixed at proposal time, while the archive may evolve before the simulation result returns.

\subsection{Steady-state update loop}\label{sec:method-algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{History-reconstructed asynchronous steady-state ABC-SMC}\label{alg:async-abc}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Evaluated history $\mathcal{H}_n$, archive size $k$, initial tolerance $\epsilon_0$
\State Reconstruct $\epsilon_{\text{hist}}$ from stored particle tolerances
\State Compute scheduler proposal $\epsilon_{\text{sched}}$
\State Set $\epsilon_n \gets \min(\epsilon_{\text{hist}}, \epsilon_{\text{sched}})$
\State Reconstruct archive $A_n$ from particles with $\rho < \epsilon_n$
\If{$|A_n| < k$}
    \State Sample from the prior
\Else
    \State Build mixture proposal $q_n(\theta)$ from $A_n$
    \State Sample a parent and perturb to obtain $\theta^\star$
    \State Clip to parameter bounds and stamp $\theta^\star$ with $\epsilon_n$
    \State Compute the importance weight associated with the proposal at creation time
\EndIf
\State Return the candidate for external simulation and discrepancy evaluation
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}
